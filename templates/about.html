There's no denying that there's a sitgma against mental health in this country. In fact, if you google just the word "stigma," an article about mental health is one of the first suggestions. In a country where one of the leading causes of death is suicide, you would think we'd take mental health a little more seriously. By some estimates, one in five people has a mental illness, diagnosed or not. Coupled with the fact that a lot of health insurance plans don't cover mental health treatment, it's most likely the latter.

Statistically speaking, the percentage of adults with diagnosed depressive disorders has been on the rise since 2010. They also tend to be diagnosed more frequently in women.





So what is a depressive disorder?

Well, it can range in severity but generally speaking a depressive disorder includes some of the following symptoms:
  • reduced concentration and attention
  • reduced self-esteem and self-confidence
  • ideas of guilt and unworthiness (even in a mild type of episode)
  • bleak and pessimistic views of the future
  • ideas or acts of self-harm or suicide
  • disturbed sleep
  • diminished appetite.

With the current COVID-19 landscape, it's easy to see how depression can set in and why mental health professionals are pushing for more research into the effect of the pandemic and social distancing.
